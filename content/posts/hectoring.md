To **hector** is to lecture someone with the visible expectation that they
should be ashamed already. Not to argue with them, not to persuade them, not
even quite to insult them: to corner them with moral certainty and keep talking.
The word names a particular flavour of domineering speech, the kind that wants
compliance more than agreement.

The word is a name. Hector was the eldest son of King Priam of Troy, the great
Trojan prince of Homer's _Iliad_, a soldier remembered for courage, restraint,
and dignity in defeat. He fought a war he knew was lost, said goodbye to his
wife and infant son in one of the tenderest scenes in ancient literature, and
was killed by Achilles outside the walls of Troy. By any reasonable measure he
was the hero of the poem. How then did his name become a verb for bullying?
Through London, in the seventeenth century.

## The Hectors of London

In the 1650s and 1660s, gangs of young rakes roamed the streets of London
picking fights, harassing passersby, and starting brawls in taverns. They styled
themselves "Hectors" with the swagger of young men who have read just enough of
the classics to flatter themselves with an ancient name. The irony was lost on
them, or perhaps deliberate: they took the name of a great defender and used it
as a flag for casual cruelty.

The name stuck to the behaviour rather than to its borrowed source. By the 1660s
"to hector" already meant to bluster, to bully, to swagger threateningly. Within
a generation the heroic Hector had been pulled down into the verb, and the word
he gave English no longer had anything to do with him. A name can be stolen this
way, and once enough mouths have used it for the wrong thing, the original owner
cannot get it back.

## The shape of hectoring

Hectoring is recognisable mostly by what it is not. It is not argument: an
argument concedes that the other side might have a point. It is not persuasion:
persuasion takes the listener's resistance seriously enough to address it. It is
not anger: anger has an end. Hectoring is the steady, righteous insistence that
the other person ought to know better, kept up past the point at which any
actual disagreement is being addressed.

The internet has been good to it. Threads, replies, quote tweets, comment
sections: each format rewards the speaker who does not stop. The hectoring tone
has migrated comfortably from the seventeenth-century tavern to the modern feed,
where the audience is larger and the listener cannot easily walk away.

The word is mostly used in disapproval, which is unusual for a verb that started
as self-aggrandisement. Calling someone a hector is itself a small refusal to be
one. There is something fitting about that. The only response the original
Hectors did not earn was the one they wanted, which was respect.
