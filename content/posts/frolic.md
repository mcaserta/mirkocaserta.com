To **frolic** is to play without a reason. Not to exercise, not to unwind, not
to network at the company picnic — to move, or laugh, or chase, or splash,
because the body found itself with energy and nowhere serious to put it. Lambs
frolic. Puppies frolic. Children, given a few minutes unsupervised near water,
frolic. Adults, mostly, have to be reminded that the option still exists.

The word is a loan and a reshape. It comes from the Dutch _vrolijk_ — "merry,
cheerful" — cousin to German _fröhlich_, and ultimately to an old Germanic root
meaning "glad" or "quick-spirited." English borrowed it in the sixteenth
century, first as an adjective meaning simply _joyous_, and only later as the
verb and noun we use now. The ancestor describes a state of mind; the descendant
describes what that state of mind makes the body do.

## The missing k

The older spelling was _frolick_, with a final _k_. It was everywhere in the
seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, then quietly lost its consonant in the
nineteenth, together with _musick_, _publick_, _logick_, and a whole small
graveyard of other _-ick_ words. Samuel Johnson's dictionary (1755) still kept
the _k_; Noah Webster, a generation later, was already pruning it. The _k_ was
not doing any phonetic work — _frolic_ and _frolick_ sound the same — so it went
the way of silent letters that nobody was emotionally attached to.

A few writers still miss the older form. The _k_ gave the word a small visible
kick, a little flick of the heel at the end, which is not the worst thing for a
verb about skipping around. Modern dictionaries list _frolic_ as the standard
and _frolick_ as an archaism. The standard won because print tends to reward
economy; the archaism survives in pastiche and period writing, where its age is
the point.

## What it protects

English has plenty of serious words for enjoying yourself: _recreation_,
_leisure_, _entertainment_, all of them vaguely clinical, each carrying a faint
scent of scheduling. _Frolic_ resists all of that. You cannot frolic on a
timetable. You cannot frolic _for_ something. The moment an activity has a
target — fitness, bonding, content creation — it stops being a frolic and
becomes an outing.

That is the small thing the word protects. Not joy in general, which is a big
category, but joy with no destination. The particular kind of pleasure that does
not survive being optimised. Every few months, on a good afternoon, it is worth
remembering that the option still has a name.
