A **rabbit hole** is the innocuous-looking opening through which you fall into a
much larger, much stranger world than you expected. You click one link, read one
footnote, watch one video, and three hours later you are reading a 1974 paper on
the breeding habits of a beetle you did not know existed, with nineteen tabs
open and no memory of what you originally sat down to do. The phrase names both
the entry point and the descent.

The image comes from the opening of Lewis Carroll's _Alice's Adventures in
Wonderland_ (1865), in which Alice follows a waistcoated White Rabbit down a
hole in a hedge and falls, slowly and dreamily, into a world governed by
different rules. Carroll's tunnel is not a threat — it is a portal. Alice is
curious, the hole is inviting, and the strange country at the bottom is where
the story actually happens. For a century the phrase stayed close to that sense:
a chosen venture into somewhere unfamiliar.

## The verb

At some point in the internet era "rabbit hole" slipped from noun to verb. To
_go down a rabbit hole_ is to follow a line of inquiry past the point where it
was serving your original purpose, into territory that is interesting on its own
terms. The phrase carries a characteristic ambivalence. It can mean you learned
something wonderful you would not have found otherwise. It can mean you lost an
afternoon. Often both.

A useful distinction:

- A **good rabbit hole** leaves you with a new map of some corner of the world —
  an unfamiliar field, a forgotten history, a craft you did not know had a
  vocabulary. The time is gone but something has been deposited.
- A **bad rabbit hole** leaves you with nothing but the residue of scrolling —
  outrage consumed, comparisons made, dopamine spent. You cannot remember what
  you read, only that you kept reading.

The shape of the descent feels similar from the inside, which is part of why the
bad kind is so effective. A recommendation algorithm optimising for engagement
has every incentive to make junk feel like Wonderland.

## The modern geometry

The hole is no longer round. Recommendation systems, autoplay queues, and
infinite feeds have turned what used to be a lateral series of chosen clicks
into a tunnel that walks for you. The old rabbit hole required curiosity and
agency — you had to keep reaching for the next link. The new one only requires
that you fail to stand up. Carroll's Alice fell because she jumped; the modern
reader falls because the floor is already moving.

What has not changed is the moment of recognition at the bottom, when you
surface and look at the clock. Sometimes it is the pleased exhaustion of having
learned something real. Sometimes it is the small, embarrassed shock of having
been fed. Learning to tell those two endings apart, in the minute before you
click the next thing, is most of what reading on the internet has become.
