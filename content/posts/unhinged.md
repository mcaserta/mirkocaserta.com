**Unhinged** literally means a door taken off its hinges. A gate, a lid, a
cabinet: something built to swing on a fixed point, now no longer attached to
it. The thing still moves, but in any direction. There is no axis. There is no
return. That is the picture the word started with, and the picture is doing all
the work.

The literal sense is recorded in English by around 1610. The figurative jump
came almost as soon as the literal sense was steady: by 1722 _unhinged_ was
already being used for a mind that had come off its frame. The metaphor must
have been instantly legible. A hinged door is a model of constrained motion: the
thing moves, but only along one trusted arc. A mind without hinges is a mind
that can swing into anything.

## From clinical to compliment

For most of its history the word was diagnostic, and not affectionately so. To
call someone unhinged was to say their reasoning had detached from whatever had
been holding it in place. The cousins were _deranged_, _unbalanced_, _broken_. A
thesaurus from 1900 would have grouped it with words a doctor used about a
patient.

That has shifted. Sometime in the last decade or so, mostly online, _unhinged_
became something you could be on purpose. "Unhinged energy", "unhinged
behaviour", "this thread is unhinged": the tone is approving. The word now
describes a particular flavour of public boldness, speaking past the hinge of
polite expectation, without obvious anchor, and finding that there is an
audience for it.

This is not new for English. Words slide between clinical and admiring all the
time. _Crazy_ runs both directions in a single sentence. _Mad_ used to mean
angry, then mentally ill, then talented (a "mad genius"). _Unhinged_ has joined
the same drift. The word still names the mechanism, a swing without an axis, but
the value attached to that swing has flipped depending on who is watching.

## The hinge that does the work

The cleverness of the metaphor sits entirely in the hinge. Take any other piece
of hardware as the source: _unscrewed_, _unbolted_, _unlatched_. The figurative
reach is much narrower. _Unscrewed_ implies sloppiness; _unbolted_ implies
escape; _unlatched_ implies access. Only the hinge gave English a way to picture
a mind that still moves but no longer along the path it was built for.

Spanish, as it happens, picked the same piece of hardware for the same metaphor:
_desquiciado_, off its _quicio_, the pivot post a door turns on. Two languages
reached for the same image to name the same thing. Sometimes a metaphor travels
well because the picture underneath is too good to pass up.

That is the small thing the word holds onto, even in its newer, friendlier
sense. The praise is never for chaos in general. It is for chaos that used to be
hinged, and is choosing not to be.
